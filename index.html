Top Ten Questions

Question 1

I'll admit that I don't exactly understand what a short sale is. Can you explain it?

Answer

In its most basic form, a short sale is when a lender agrees to accept a payoff from a homeowner of less than what is owed on the mortgage. In the current environment, this is happening because home prices declined and some homeowners are underwater on their mortgages — meaning they owe more to the lender than what the house is worth.

Usually, lenders prefer short sales to foreclosures because it means they'll likely recoup more of the money they're owed.

I'll note that being upside-down on your mortgage doesn't necessarily mean you'll be eligible for a short sale. Lenders will want to see some sign of hardship — that you can no longer afford your mortgage — along with the negative equity situation you're facing.

And I'll start this Q&A discussion by saying that it's imperative that you consult your legal and tax professionals regarding your personal situation. While I'm highlighting some generalities and commonalities in the current environment, your own decision to buy or sell as a short sale or foreclosure should be based on the advice of those familiar with your personal circumstances.

Question 2

What kind of discount can a buyer expect in a short sale?

Answer

Generally, lenders are in the driver's seat on short sales and they aim to garner as much as they can for the property. They know they won't be receiving the full amount of what the current homeowner owes on his or her mortgage, but lenders like to receive fair market value for the home.

What this means for the buyer is that an acceptable short sale offer is generally reflective of today's current home price environment. This will be down significantly from the peak of a few years ago — you certainly aren't likely to pay what the previous homeowner did for the house.

You may be able to get the house at a slight discount — after all, fair market value is a range. A short sale won't get a home at a deeply-discounted price, but you may find yourself at the low end of the fair market value range.

Question 3

I'm in the market for a home and it seems that almost half of all the listings I look at online are distressed properties. How much of the market do short sales and foreclosures really make up?

Answer

In some areas of the greater D.C. region, short sales make up as much as 40 percent of the market — and as much as half of the available inventory in some areas. This varies greatly by market as some areas have relatively few short sales available, and others have quite a few options.

This is a great opportunity for well-qualified buyers in our region. If you have the time to dedicate to a short sale transaction and realistic expectations regarding the process, you may have the option of pursuing a significant percentage of the current available inventory in your area — and perhaps walking away with an ideal purchase price on your next home.

Question 4

What are short sales and foreclosures really doing to home prices in this area?

Answer

The truth of the matter is that it really depends on what's happening in your local market. There are a few things that distressed properties can do to a local housing market — but the degree to which the impact is negative depends entirely on what the rest of the market looks like. If short sales are a larger percentage of the total homes sold, or if local appraisers incorporate a certain percentage of distressed assets into their data profile when completing appraisals, it can certainly impact the dynamic of the local housing market where you are.

I'll say this, though, if you're tapped into what's happening in your local market — and the free neighborhood-level Long & Foster Market Minute reports are a quick-and-easy way to learn about housing data in your area — you may see where distressed assets in the mix of the housing offerings can present some interesting opportunities.

If you're in a position to buy a home, for example, you'll likely find that distressed assets offer you some options with pricing in your favor.

For those who are trying to refinance, distressed assets impacting housing prices can be a frustrating situation, but as we continue to work through these properties, the inventory will eventually shift back to a majority of traditional sales, and pricing will likely start a reasonable path of recalibration.

Question 7

Why is going through the hassle of a short sale better for a homeowner than foreclosing or just walking away?

Answer

I understand that what has happened in the real estate industry in the last several years is unprecedented. Times are difficult for so many families in our area, and across the country, and it may seem like the best option is to foreclose.

But I believe there will come a time in the not-too-distant future when many homeowners who are currently struggling are in a much better economic situation. Perhaps you'll get a better job or receive a promotion. Then you'll be glad you took the time to learn about your options when comparing a short sale to foreclosure

.

A short sale packs less of a punch on your credit, and in many cases, you'll be able to purchase your next home in as few as two or three years after completing your short sale. With a foreclosure, the waiting period could be five years, seven years or longer, depending on the lender and your personal situation.

Lenders are also starting to levy judgments against sellers that remain tied to you as the seller after the deal is completed. These lenders are looking to recoup their losses. So if you foreclose, the amount you'd be required to pay back — the amount equaling the bank's loss — is likely to be much greater than if you successfully executed a short sale. Not only is a foreclosure sold for less, which means the bank will still be owed more, but there's also additional fees associated with foreclosures, such as paying for attorneys for the lenders

.

Once again, I want to add that you should definitely consult your legal and tax professionals regarding your personal situation. Question 8

Who is eligible to participate in a short sale?

Answer

To engage in a short sale transaction with your lender, you're going to need to prove that you're facing some sort of hardship. That doesn't mean you're late on your payments, necessarily, but you'll need to indicate to the bank why you're interested in a short sale.

An experienced Realtor will be able to offer you guidance relevant to your particular situation, but in general, a hardship letter — which will likely be required for a short sale — should point out the reason behind your request. This could be a loss of a job, a pending divorce or relocation for a job. It could also be because of an illness or medical bills, or an adjustable-rate mortgage that's about to reset to a much higher interest rate.

You may also be asked to show your lack of other assets available to pay for the mortgage.

The key, as I understand it, is to be as factual and concise as possible. While the reason for your request may be personal in nature, what you're requesting is a change to the terms of your contract — not an approval to not pay your mortgage.

Question 9

Are there complications in getting financing approved for a distress sale? Any tips you could offer?

Answer

There can be hurdles because you're dealing with a distressed property — and not all of the hurdles are directly related to lenders and financing issues. For example, if the house has been vacant for a while, the utilities might have been shut off, which will hinder a home inspection process. If there are issues of disrepair, the seller is likely not to have the funds to repair anything, and the seller's lender won't pay for certain repairs either. If it's a repair that's necessary for health and safety reasons — like a banister that's missing or broken — you may have some luck, but you're unlikely to have success asking for that old, dirty carpet to be replaced.

Your lender may not start working on your financing and underwriting until a short sale has been approved, but then there may be a rush to get underwriting completed in time for closing. It's a tricky situation that can be overcome with diligence and attention to detail — and a lot of follow-up on your part to make sure you understand how your lender is prepared to proceed with your case.

Talk to your lender, but you may find that being pre-qualified for financing may help your progress with distressed properties.
